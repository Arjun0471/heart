How Common Are These Conditions?

Congenital heart defects (CHDs) are the most common birth anomalies in children globally.

  • 1 in every 100 children worldwide is born with a CHD — that's around 4.18 million children under five globally in 2021.
  • About 13.5 lakh babies per year are born with CHDs globally.
  • Globally, critical heart defects make up nearly 25% of all CHDs.

What Is a Congenital Heart Defect?

A CHD is a structural problem in the heart present from birth. They may involve:

  • ASD (Atrial Septal Defect): hole between upper heart chambers (~10-15% of CHDs)
  • VSD (Ventricular Septal Defect): hole between lower chambers (20-60%, most common)
  • PDA (Patent Ductus Arteriosus): remaining fetal vessel—5-10% of CHDs
  • TOF (Tetralogy of Fallot): complex cyanotic defect—accounts for 7-10% of CHDs
  • HLHS (Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome): underdeveloped left heart—2-3% of CHDs

Why Early Detection Matters

Early diagnosis improves outcomes:

  • Newborn screening and fetal echocardiograms can detect serious defects early.
  • Timely treatment means non-critical CHDs have up to 97% one-year survival; critical ones up to 83%.
  • Latest data shows global CHD prevalence remains around 6.4 per 1,000 children under five.

Signs You Should Know

  • Cyanosis (blue lips or skin)
  • Fast or labored breathing, especially during feeding
  • Poor weight gain or frequent lung infections
  • Excessive sweating while eating or crying
  • Detectable heart murmur

Treatment & Care

Depending on the defect, options include:

  • Non-invasive monitoring
  • Medications
  • Catheter-based procedures
  • Minimally invasive and open-heart surgeries

Thanks to modern medicine, many children with CHDs grow into healthy adults.

Living with a CHD

With treatment, survival has greatly improved:

  • Non-critical CHDs: 97% survival through first year
  • Critical CHDs: ~83% one-year survival, increasing over past decades
  • CHDs are now the leading cause of congenital birth defect deaths, but global mortality has declined 56% since 1990

Where to Learn More

  • Consult a pediatric cardiologist
  • Look for CHD support groups—like HeartKids, CHD associations
  • Access trusted sources: CDC, WHO, and peer-reviewed studies
